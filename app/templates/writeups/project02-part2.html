Difficult Problems, Practical Solutions

Getting my first useful application up and running

The Templates

Email templates are surely not the most complicated piece of software you can imagine, but they are incredible useful tools. I'm writing this a little over a week since I've "completed" most of the template scripts for my application and I've been making use of them as often and I can at work. They definitely save me a lot of time and create a base level of consistency for many of the boilerplate emails the Customer Service department has to send. There are many, many ways a programmer can accomplish this same functionality, but Python and Flask work just fine for my purposes. That's not to say I've run into my share of difficulties along the way.

Sending the Email

Setting up the actual input fields - HTML forms rendered by the Flask-WTF extension - is the subject of Miguel Grinberg's third article in his Flask Mega Tutorial. As I was already familiar with grabbing the data field of an HTML form element from my date checker app, after the forms were set up it was time to decide what method I was going to use to actually send the emails the app would be composing. As you'd expect, there are quite a few options to choose from.


SMTP

As I began my Google journey in search of some direction for this next step, the most prevalent suggestion was to use SMTP. SMTP is such a popular and preferred way of sending email in Python that there's an example implementation in the official documentation. Naturally I was pretty excited to have apparently arrived at my answer after so little searching effort. However, I quickly discovered that SMTP wouldn't work for my program because of how my colleagues and myself would be accessing it.

Creating a self-installing executable file for my application with py2exe wasn't really an option because of my company's IT/administration rights policy, so I needed the app to live somewhere it could be accessed even without a Python interpreter on the user's local machine. This was a big factor in my decision to host CSTools on PythonAnywhere. Since the template script would be run on a different server than the local machine the user would have to enter their email authentication credentials every time they wanted to use one of my templates. I decided that this was a bit too clunky and slow for what I had envisioned.

Mailto

The next contender was trusty old mailto. I remembered this guy from my first primitive forays into web development something like 15 years ago (on my slick Tripod/GeoCities sites, no doubt). This familiarity gave me pause - surely something a novice dev was familiar with fifteen years ago has been deprecated and superseded by better techniques at this point. I wasn't far off, as there are a few issues with mailto that lead many to call for its deprecation. These issues are discussed in the previous Wiki link, but briefly they come down to issues surrounding a scenario in which the user does not have a default mail program configured and security, as a mailto URL can openly display email addresses making them visible to scamming bots.
